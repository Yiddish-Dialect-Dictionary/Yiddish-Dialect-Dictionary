ETYMOLOGY
===========
{DW¹ notes that many German dialects preserved the short -a- - not in Yiddish (except in westernmost Western Yiddish?).
Interesting that Yiddish does not preserve the umlaut in the plural, which arose in MHG and came to dominate in NHG. Maybe this indicates that the word was not used much, or in order to maintain a distinction from פֿעטער 'uncle', which would sound the same in Northeastern Yiddish.}
Got. fadar, ON faðir, OE fæder, English father, OS fadar, Dutch vader, OHG fatar, MHG vater, NHG Vater < PGmc. *fader- < PIE *ph₂-tér- (or *pətḗr); a hysterokinetic ter-stem: *ph₂-tér, (gen.) *ph₂-tr-ós, (acc.) *ph₂-tér-m. The word referred to the public role of the father as head of the household; another more familiar term was also used in many language (see טאַטע).
Related: Sanskrit pitā́, pitár- Greek πᾰτήρ, (gen.) πᾰτρός, Latin pater, patris,
