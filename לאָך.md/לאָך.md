'hole'

ETYMOLOGY
===========
The meaning 'backwater, middle of nowhere' may be borrowed from Polish dziura 'hole', which has a similar meaning. 
Got. us-luk* 'opening,' ON lok 'end', OE loc 'lock', OHG loh < PGmc. *-luk-a-ⁿ / *luk-a-; nomen actionis to PGmc. *lūk- (cf. Got. uslukan 'to open,' ON lúka, OE lūkan) < PIE *lū + -g-
{Bammesberger 1990: 57}
Vowel 41, Proto-Yiddish *ɔ
{Katz 1982: 58}

Loch; lescher {HOLLAND, Beem, Jerosche}
	•	Er komt bei die drei lescher. 'He comes late to shul. The three lescher (from loch) are: Mie jidmee loch mie jischwee loch umie ja'aroch loch - from the Nishmat prayer tha comes right before Borchu. So he is just in the nick of time.' {Jerosche №564}
	•	Wen e loch in die brik is, falt der arme man dorch. 'If there is a hole in the bridge, the poor man will fall through. Misfortune always happens to the unlucky one. Cf. 'n arm man is n' schlemiel.' {Jerosche №565}
