ETYMOLOGY
===========
{Syncretic from NHG fliegen and fliehen?
DW² notes that the forms of fliegen and fliehen tended to be confused in all historical periods, but were more systematically differentiated from the 17th c.
Wenker's Sprachatlas map 5 shows that some parts of East Central German have flie- rather than flieg-, even in Brandenburg east of Berlin; also a very large area around, south, and west of Frankfurt am Main, incl. Mainz and Wiesbaden. Perhaps the latter is the source for the Yiddish form?}
ON fljúga, OE flēogan, English to fly, Dutch vliegen, OHG fliugan, fliogan, MHG vliegen, NHG fliegen < PGmc. *fleugan- 'to fly' (or *fleug-a-) < *fleuhan- (perhaps to disambiguate from *þleuhan- 'to flee') < *pléuk-e- (NEUR), an extension of the PIE root *pleu- 'to flow, swim', perhaps itself an extension of *pel- 'to flow'.
Related: Lithuanian plaũkti 'to swim, float', plùnksna 'feather'.
Got. þliuhan, OE flēon, English to flee, OS fliohan, OHG fliohan, MHG vliehen, NHG fliehen < PGmc. *þleuhan- 'to flee' (or *fleuhan-) < *tléuk-e- (GM); probably unrelated to *fleugan- (but perhaps Got. þl- < fl-).
{DW² 9: 627, 635; Kluge/Seebold 2011: 302, 303; Kroonen 2013: 144, 544; Lexer 3: 401}
