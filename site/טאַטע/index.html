    טאַטע ־ס דער 'father'

    ETYMOLOGY

    {The EY form (usually with short /a/) probably comes from Polish tata, while the WY (or Czech Y) with long /aː/ is probably from Czech táta.} Beider suggests that intimate family vocabulary is likely to come from the West Cnaanic (Old Czech) substrate. Polish tata; cf. Czech táta; Ukrainian та́то; Belarusian та́та; < Proto-Balto-Slavic tatas < PIE tatás; cf. Vedic Sanskrit tatá, Greek (vocative) τατᾶ, (Homer) τέττα, Latin tata, Romanian tată, Lithuanian tė̃tis, Russian тя́тя, NHG Tatta, tatte, tate {in DW¹!} {Beider 2015: 451; Chernykh 2: 278; DW¹ 21: 160; Pokorny 3: 1056; Vasmer 4,141} The idiom ווי בײַם טאַטן אין ווײַנגאָרטן ...

    PHRASEOLOGY טאַטע אין הימל 'father in heaven! (vocative)', translation of the Hebrew אָבִינוּ שֶׁבַּשָּׁמַיִם Coincidentally matches the ancient Indo-European name for the sky god, PIE *di̯ḗu̯s ph₂tḗr, cf. Latin Jūpiter, Diēspiter, Sanskrit djáuṣ pitā́, Greek (vocative) Ζεῦ πάτερ
