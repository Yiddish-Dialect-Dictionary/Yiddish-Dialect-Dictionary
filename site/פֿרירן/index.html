    פֿרירן געפֿרוירן/געפֿראָרן 'to freeze'

    ETYMOLOGY

    {Why the alternative past participles with /o/ or /oj/? The /o/ may reflect MHG gevrorn when the root syllable was closed.} Herzog writes that the alternation {gefrojrn/gefrejrn} is typical of vowel 42 (MHG vrœren, past participle gevrœrt 'to cause to freeze'), while {gefrorn} suggests vowel 41 (/o/ in a closed syllable). He concludes that there was a merger in Yiddish of the transitive and intransitive forms of the verb (MHG vriesen, p.p. gevrorn and vrœrn, p.p.gevrœrt) and that, though the final nasal constitutes a separate syllable in Yiddish, it most sometimes be considered, historically, as syllable closing. {Almost certainly so in WY and in earlier periods of the language.} {Herzog 1964: 264}

    ON frjósa, OE frēosan, English to freeze, Dutch vriezen, (past participle) gevroren, OHG friosan, (past participle) gifroren, MHG vriesen, (past participle) gevrorn, NHG frieren < PGmc. freusan- 'to freeze' (or freusa-, or frews-) (with Verner alternation freusan-, (past participle) fruzana-) < préus-e- (WEUR) (or preu̯s-). (Kluge/Seebold thinks freusa- originally meant 'to spray', from a PIE root preus-.) The original past participle was frusta- < *prus-to- (see פֿראָסט). Perhaps related Latin pruīna 'frost', Sanskrit pruṣvā́- 'dew drop', {DW² 9: 1082; EWA 3: 570; Kluge/Seebold 2011: 318; Kroonen 2013: 154; Lexer 3: 516}
